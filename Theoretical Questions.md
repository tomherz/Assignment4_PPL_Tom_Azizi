## Question 1 - Lazy lists

### a. Equivalence criterion for two lazy lists

1. **Base Case (Empty Lists):** If both lists are empty, they are equivalent

2. **Inductive Step (Non-Empty Lists):** If both lists are non-empty, they are equivalent if and only if their heads (current elements) are strictly equal, and the Tails of the lists (obtained by evaluating their tails) are also equivalent


*Note: If one list is empty and the other is not, they are not equivalent

### b. Equivalence proof for even-square-1 and even-square-2
Since both procedures process the infinite stream generated by integers-from(0), neither list is ever empty. Therefore, the "Empty Lists" case from our definition in section (a) is trivially inapplicable. To prove that the lists are equivalent, we apply the "Non-Empty Lists" criterion and show that for any integer $n \ge 0$, their heads are strictly equal and the Tails of the lists (obtained by evaluating their tails) are also equivalent. 

We prove this by induction on the integer $n$:

1. **Base Case ($n = 0$):** Since $0$ is an even number, both procedures evaluate as follows:
   * **Heads:** In even-square-1, $0$ is squared to $0^2=0$, which is even and passes the filter, so the head is $0$. In even-square-2, $0$ passes the filter and is then squared to $0$, so the head is $0$. Their heads are strictly equal.
   * **Tails:** Evaluating their tails forces the deferred computation, moving both procedures synchronously to process the next integer, $n = 1$. By induction, these Tails are equivalent.

2. **Inductive Step:** Assuming the lists are equivalent up to an integer $n$, we evaluate the next step based on the parity of $n$:
   * **Case 1 - $n$ is even:** 
      *  **Heads:** In even-square-1, $n$ is squared to $n^2$ (which is even) and passes the filter. In even-square-2, $n$ passes the filter and is then squared to $n^2$. Thus, both heads are equal to $n^2$.
     * **Tails:** Evaluating their tails forces the next step, moving both procedures to process the next integer, $n+1$. By the induction hypothesis, these remaining portions are equivalent.
   * **Case 2 - $n$ is odd:** Both procedures synchronously skip the current element and immediately reduce to processing the next integer ($n+1$):
     * even-square-1 computes $n^2$ (which is odd) and the filter skips it, moving to $n+1$.
     * even-square-2 filters out $n$ immediately because it is odd, moving to $n+1$.

*Note: Since both procedures align perfectly on the base case, match on every even step, and synchronously skip every odd step, they generate the exact same infinite sequence of values $(0, 4, 16, 36, \dots)$. Therefore, even-square-1 and even-square-2 are equivalent.*

### a. Equivalence criterion for a procedure and its Success-Fail-Continuations (SFC) version
To define the equivalence, we specify the types of the original procedure $f$ and its SFC version $f_{sfc}$:
* $f: T_1 \to T_2 \cup \{'\text{fail}\}$
* $f_{sfc}: T_1 \times (T_2 \to T_3) \times (\text{Empty} \to T_4) \to T_3 \cup T_4$

The procedures $f$ and $f_{sfc}$ are equivalent if and only if for any input $x \in T_1$ and continuations succ and fail, the following conditions hold:

1. **Success Case:** If the original procedure succeeds and returns a value $v \neq '\text{fail}$, the SFC version applies the succ continuation to $v$:
   $$\text{if } f(x) = v \implies f_{sfc}(x, succ, fail) = succ(v)$$

2. **Failure Case:** If the original procedure fails and returns 'fail, the SFC version applies the fail continuation:
   $$\text{if } f(x) = '\text{fail} \implies f_{sfc}(x, succ, fail) = fail()$$


### d. Equivalence proof for get-value and get-value$
We prove that get-value ($f$) and get-value$ ($f_{sfc}$) are equivalent according to our definition by induction on the structure of assoc-list:

   * The SFC version evaluates the fail continuation: 
     $$f_{sfc}(\text{'()}, \text{key}, succ, fail) = fail()$$

     * By the induction hypothesis, these remaining computations are equivalent.

## Question 3 - Logic programming


### 1. unify [t(s(s), G, s, p, t(K), s), t(s(G), G, s, p, t(K), U)]
* **Algorithm Steps:**
  1. The outer functor t is identical on both sides, so we proceed to compare the arguments from left to right.
  2. Argument 1: Compare s(s) with s(G). The functor s is identical, so we compare their inner components: the constant s against the variable G. This results in the substitution: G = s.
  3. Argument 2: Compare variable G with variable G. Since we substituted G = s, this reduces to comparing s with s, which matches perfectly.
  4. Argument 3: Constant s against constant s – identical.
  5. Argument 4: Constant p against constant p – identical.
  6. Argument 5: Expression t(K) against t(K) – completely identical.
  7. Argument 6: Compare constant s with variable U. This results in the substitution: U = s.

* **Result:** Success.
* **Substitutions Found:** G = s, U = s.

### 2. unify [g(1, M, g, G, U, g, v(M)), g(1, v(U), g, v(M), v(G), g, v(M))]
* **Algorithm Steps:**
  1. The outer functor g is identical on both sides, so we compare argument-by-argument.
  2. Argument 1: Constant 1 against 1 – identical.
  3. Argument 2: Variable M against structure v(U). Since M does not appear inside the structure, this is a valid substitution: M = v(U).
  4. Argument 3: Constant g against constant g – identical.
  5. Argument 4: Variable G against structure v(M). We substitute the value of M found in step 3 (M = v(U)) and get the substitution: G = v(v(U)).
  6. Argument 5: Variable U against structure v(G). We substitute the value of G found in step 5 (G = v(v(U))) and get the equation: U = v(v(v(U))).
  7. Occurs Check: The algorithm detects that the variable U appears inside the structure it is being compared to.

* **Result:** Failure.
* **Explanation:** Occurs Check Failure. A variable cannot be unified with a structure that contains itself, as it would create an infinite recursive structure.

### 4. unify [p([v | [V | VV]]), p([[v | V] | VV])]
* **Algorithm Steps:**
  1. The outer functor p is identical, so we move inside to compare the list arguments.
  2. Compare the first element (Head) of both lists:
     * In the left list, the first element is the atomic constant v.
     * In the right list, the first element is itself a compound list: [v | V].
  3. The algorithm attempts to unify the constant v with the list structure [v | V].

* **Result:** Failure.
* **Explanation:** Structure Clash Failure. An atomic constant (v) cannot be unified with a compound list structure ([v | V]).
